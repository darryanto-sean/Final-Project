Experience
In the fall of my first year at Santa Monica College, I formed a team with another student to participate in the first HackCC: a hackathon tailored for Southern California community college students. Coming into the event, the only CS languages I knew were Java from the high school course and Objective-C, but throughout the hackathon, I learned so much more about Java and Python. While at HackCC, I worked on an application service for the application Yo, a one-click communication application. My service allowed the user to send a ‘Yo’ to a WellsFargoFinder which collected the location to open a yelp page of the nearest Wells Fargo.
In the Spring, I joined a team at the AT&T: Hack to the Future to develop a piece of hardware aptly named Swych. The theme of the hackathon was to further the development of the Internet of Things, which is seen in the general trend of smart appliances that interact with users, and Swych did just that. It was a product that allowed users to check the status of various appliances that were connected to Swych and switch the power status of each individually. What was innovative about Swych was that it turned ordinary appliances into “smart” appliances, and did not require a phone with Internet capabilities. My team consisted of 4 programmers and 1 electrical engineer. Our electrical engineer developed the hardware behind Swych, primarily using Intel’s Edison board. I was in charge of programming the code for the client to server communication with Node.js We won first place in that hackathon.
In my second year of college I became the website coordinator for Alpha Gamma Sigma, an Honors Society at Santa Monica College. I was reached out by a friend to help create, maintain and host the website for the club because the coordinator at the time would be transferring after that semester. I decided to take the position as website coordinator and stayed for the entire year. Since first taking on the position, I completely revamped the website so that members and volunteer organizations could interact with the executive board with an easier and more aesthetically pleasing interface. SMC is the only chapter in California with a website created by an executive board member.
At the end beginning of my last semester at Santa Monica College, my coworkers from the Computer Science Lab at school and I participated at the ATT Hack in Irvine, CA. We created an application called Tutr that allowed students to search for tutors in their area similar to the service that Uber provides. It was a project that we were all interested in considering few of us had any real experience with the coding required to bring the product to life. My friend Bilguun and I were in charge of the back-end programming which involved creating and setting up the webserver, to networking the client to the server. With only 24 hours, we were able to complete the front-end and back-end, but ran out of time before connecting the server to the client. Although the project ended with failure, I gained an immeasurable amouont of experience with back-end that I seeked so fervently.
